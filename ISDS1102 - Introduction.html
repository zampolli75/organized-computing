Introduction: Practical Computing Skills

Introduction

Proficiency with Information Technology (IT) is a MUST for any professional in any discipline. You are enrolled in college because you want to become a knowledge worker: “A person whose occupation is predominantly concerned with generating or interpreting information, as contrasted with manual labor.”1 Knowledge workers in any profession, from researchers in labs, to journalists on the front lines, to nurses in hospitals, leverage computers and digital technology to improve their productivity and the quality of their work. If you are in the college of business, you know that Information Technology has, over the last thirty years, revolutionized every aspect of business, from Accounting to Strategy.

As Steve Jobs – the co-founder of Apple, Inc. – observed in 19902: “the computer is the most remarkable tool that we have ever come up with. It is the equivalent of a bicycle for our minds.”

What he meant is that just like a person on a bicycle can go further and go faster than a person walking, a computer can help your mind work faster and go further than you would achieve alone. As a knowledge worker you will be doing most of your work with your mind.

Your goal in this course is to take advantage, rather than being hurt, by the continuing shift toward the digitization of work. If you successfully complete the course you will become what we call a sophisticated end-user. In four months you will be a savvy knowledge worker by developing expertise in three distinct but related domains: Practical computing skills, foundational information technology concepts, and foundational information systems concept. This class is your chance to learn to ride the bicycle effectively and efficiently.

Description

The practical computing skills portion of this course focuses specifically on accomplishing intermediate and advanced competences in the two most important applications in the Microsoft Office suite of productivity tools: Microsoft Word and Microsoft Excel. As you know Word is a “word processor” and it is used to compose and manage text documents. Excel is designed to create and manage spreadsheets. The first good reason to apply yourself in this course, is that mastery of spreadsheets and word processors will get you a job. Consider just one statistic from a recent report by the research firm Burning Glass Technologies: More than 8 in 10 middle-skill jobs (82%) require digital skills, a 4% increase since 2015: Spreadsheets and word processing remain the baseline for digital skills, with 78% of middle-skill jobs calling for these at a minimum.

Microsoct Word and Excel are not the only two commercial word processor and spreadsheet software available today. It is legitimate for you to ask why we focus on these two products. The reason is simple, Microsoft Office dominates the market, and it has been doing so for over 27 years (see Figure 1)3. Put it another way, the probability that when you get your first internship or your first job your company will use Microsoft Office is overwhelming. If you need to make an impact as a knowledge worker in that organization Microsoft Office is the application that you want to master. What you will learn in this course will help you be more efficient and effective in all your future classes and throughout your career. It is clearly in your best interest to take the course seriously!

Not convinced yet? Here is another reason. Microsoft Word and Excel are not only widely adopted in organizations across the world, but they are also an example of commercial off-the-shelf software. The designation “commercial off-the-shelf” (COTS) refers to software and hardware products that, unlike custom-developed applications, are readily available for purchase to the public. Historically software was literally available “off-the-shelf” of a retail store (see Figure 2).

Today the name has remained but you will generally download the COTS software you need from a major app store or the vendor’s own website.

COTS software, by its very nature, is designed to appeal to the largest possible audience of users. That means that in the case of Word and Excel, Microsoft has invested millions of men-hours to create the widest possible array of functionalities in these applications over the years. Microsoft Word and Excel, while relatively simple to learn and use, are very powerful. However, most users only utilize a tiny fraction of these functionalities – by some estimates just 5 commands (paste, save, copy, undo, bold) account for 32% of the total commands used!4

Few users have the luxury to engage in advanced training (you should consider yourself lucky to have to take this class). Thus, even the limited functionality they utilize they typically have learned from their colleagues or friends. It is a well-known fact that vicarious learning of software, as it is formally called, is substandard. Imitation breeds bad habits. Like a game of Telephone5 bad habits get compounded as friends teach each other down the line. Telephone is funny, but being inefficient in using the instrument with which you do your work is not that funny! If you apply yourself in this class you will not be hostage to the deleterious dynamics of vicarious software learning. You have the opportunity to learn intermediate and advanced features of Word and Excel and you have the opportunity to learn directly from an expert source. Mastery is what this course offers, rather than mimicry. Take advantage!

Not convinced yet? Here is another reason. This may be one of the courses with the highest payoff in your whole college career. As a knowledge worker you will spend most of your time collecting, processing, and disseminating information. In this class you will learn about 100 skills, and you will learn to be efficient in how you perform tasks that require these skills (i.e., you will perform these tasks more efficiently than you would have if you did not take the class). The typical knowledge worker spends 25 hours a week in front of the computer. If 20% of this time is spent working in Word or Excel and you can save 40% of the time you would take without this class, over a 30-year career your time savings amount to 17,472 hours, or 728 days, or 104 weeks… that’s exactly two years! Assume that you take your work seriously, and you spend 10 hours a week on the practical computing skills portion of the course (14 weeks), your return on the investment is 145 hours in savings for every hour of work (more than a 14,000% ROI). And, by the way, our computation above does not include second order effects. That is, if you take this course seriously you will be better equipped to learn other software applications that we don’t explicitly cover in class (e.g., Microsoft Power Point). You will also develop some general knowledge about efficient and effective computer use that will apply to any computing work you will do in the future.

Assumptions

There are two basic assumptions underpinning our design of the practical computing skills portion of the course:

  1. You have basic computing skills
  2. You have the commitment and competence to use online resources to self-learn

Basic Skills

You have been using computers for the majority of your life. Therefore, there are a number of important computer skills that we assume you have already mastered. We listed them below, if you don’t feel comfortable with any of these make sure that you acquire them immediately.

  • You have some prior knowledge with Microsoft Office applications in general and with Word and Excel specifically. While our learning material starts from the very basics of each applications, we assume you already know how to create a basic Word Document and Excel Worksheet. We also take it as given that you can quickly self-learn any of the basic skills that you may be missing.
  • You are able to use your prior knowledge of computing to navigate the small potential differences that can occur between available instructions and the specifics of your application. Software programs are deceptively complex. They have thousands of features and functionalities. Your specific application may have some special configurations or customization. We assume that these minor inconsistencies will not stop you cold in your tracks. We take it as given that you can either navigate these differences independently or seek appropriate help to correct them.
  • You have the basic file management skills of a functional computer users. Specifically, you know how to organize your files in meaningful storage hierarchies that make it easy to find them, and you know how to use conventional and intuitive naming conversions for them. You understand the role of file extensions, and you can manage them appropriately.
  • You understand the importance of saving your work and backing up your files. Specifically, we take it as given that during a working session (e.g., completing your homework) you remember to save after completing any substantive task. This is because you understand that primary memory is volatile and that computers can, and do, freeze from time to time. Thus, were the computer to lose power for any reason, your unsaved work would be lost. We also take it as given that you appreciate the importance of backups, whether on peripherals (e.g., a USB drive) or remotely (e.g., on a cloud service like Dropbox). This is because you understand that secondary storage devices are subject to equipment failure. Albeit rare, when equipment failure happens it can be very costly. Thus, the appropriate risk management strategy is to develop sound backup practices. In the case of this course you should have at least two separate copies of each important file at all times.
  • You have basic time management skills and you know how to successfully organize your work. One day you will be a manager, which means you will be responsible for organizing the work and efforts of your employees, in some cases, hundreds or even thousands of them. At this stage in your professional life you are very lucky, all you have to do is organize your own self. We take it as given that you can do so. If you find yourself unable to do so reach out to us immediately.
  • If any of the above assumptions is not correct in your specific case, or if any of the terms and concepts we refer to in the above section is foreign to you, it is your responsibility to contact a teaching assistant as early as possible and remedy your current skill deficiency.

Our Pedagogical Approach: The Flipped Classroom

Practical computing skills are widely distributed amongst the population of students in this class. Some of you have considerable experience with Microsoft Office and may have taken formal courses in high school. Sometimes these courses are fairly advanced. Many of you have limited knowledge of Word and Excel, knowledge that comes from independently working in the application and learning from friends.

With such a variety of skills in the course the flipped classroom is the optimal approach to ensure a high degree of sophistication of all participants by the end of the course, while maintaining peak efficiency for each of you. The flipped classroom concept prescribes that high-quality self-directed learning modules be made available to the learners, along with target learning outcomes and a schedule of achievement expectations.

We use a variety of learning material for Microsoft Office, including the high-quality online resources made available by Microsoft. We supplement these materials with concepts and skills described in this packet. Each module ends with a practice homework developed by us, specifically for you. The practice document establishes target learning outcomes. For each assignment we provide a sample file that represents the “solution” to the homework that you can use to verify the quality of your own efforts.

Armed with these three tools each of you can organize the learning schedule as needed, depending on your prior knowledge. If you are already very advanced you could skim the materials to make sure that you do know it and that you are aware of the many different approaches available in the application to perform the same task. Doing so enables you to verify that you have not picked up any bad habits and inefficient approaches in your previous experiences. If you are new to the application or the particular set of skills required to perform the week’s tasks, you should allocate significant time to review and practice the available material. Some words of caution and suggestions:

  • For as advanced as you may be, chances are you can learn quite a bit from the material we have created. There are often many ways to perform the same task in software applications. Reviewing the materials may show you new approaches that make you even more effective and efficient. For the record, we believe you will pick up many of these valuable tips, no matter how advanced you are already.
  • As mentioned above, you may be able to perform the task while being completely unaware that, because of vicarious learning, you picked up your friends’ bad habits. Thus, while you could complete the homework without reviewing the material, you may be nowhere near as efficient as you should be. This inefficiency will be very costly during the exam, where you are required to complete many tasks under time constraint. More importantly, these bad habits will hold you back in your career, where efficiency is a premium skill of the modern knowledge worker.

The ability to organize your learning is a great advantage also for those of you that come to the course without a strong background in Microsoft Word and Excel. The material is available to you 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. You can repeat a unit as many times as you need, and you can return to the material while you complete the homework.

Of course, while we have high quality self-directed learning material available to you, we are here to enable your learning. On days when we have practical skills class sessions you should attend class. We will cover some of the most difficult skills and provide some tips and suggestions. Keeping with the flipped classroom model however we will leave enough time for you to practice independently while we are available to answer questions and help you in completing your work.

As you can see from this design of our pedagogy, there is one big assumption behind our approach: That you are a mature adult, conscious of the investment of time and money you are making in your education. We count on you to realize that you are doing homework for yourself, not for us. We are eager and happy to help you, but you are in charge and responsible for your own success.

Basis Mechanics

Microsoft Office 2016

We believe that a practical skills course should allow you to use whatever computer and operating systems you deem appropriate. When this course was first introduced at LSU in 1994, over 90% of all personal computers in the market ran on the Wintel platform6. Today, the operating systems market share is more even. While Windows still dominates with 83%, Apple’s OSX has a sizable 9.61% of the market.

We don’t believe it is reasonable to force you to use a computer different than the one you own. We do however need to engage in version control in order to be efficient. Thus, we require that in this course you use the most recent version of Microsoft Word and Excel for your operating system. This year it is Microsoft Office 2016 for both Windows and Macintosh computers. It is furthermore required to update the software release, so check for available updates before the first Microsoft Office session. Using the same release and version will provide consistent software across all computers in the classroom, preventing us to deal with bugs or errors unique to some releases.

LSU provides you with free license for Microsoft Office by virtue of your LSU enrollment. Following the instructions on the Tigerware website you will be able to get your license and install Microsoft Office (IMPORTANT: you must chose a version under RECOMMENDED VERSION). If it’s the first time you visit Tigerware, you will notice that you have available other free licensed software as well. We suggest you to go through the available software as you might find some tools that might boost your productivity during your career at LSU (e.g. citation management, project management tools).

Defending from Viruses

The files present in your computer are the outcome of many hours of your effort. It is your duty to have the right security on your computer to protect such valuable documents. There is a plethora of different virus and other malware categories, each of which excels in causing damage (e.g. deleting files, making programs crash unexpectedly, stealing information, slowing down the performance of your machine).

We will discuss malware and computer security in the course. At this stage however it is critical that you make sure your computer is protected. LSU provides you with a free license for Symantec Protection and other useful software to secure your computer. You can download it from Tigerware.

On Tigerware you also have free access to the Cisco VPN client, which will allows you to connect to the LSU network remotely. Using publicly accessible Wi-Fi hotspots, such as the ones you find in coffee shops or airports, is as dangerous as surfing dubious websites without any antivirus. Connecting to the LSU network through the VPN will make your connection secure. Connecting to the LSU network when off campus provides you with other benefits, such as accessing journals, newspapers, and resources that LSU grants to its members.

Backing Up Your Files

We advise you to back up your files to protect your work from unnecessary loss. We suggest two main options:

  1. External Hard Drives: A flash drive or a hard disk offers the simplest option to create an entire back up of your computer. Both Windows and Mac users have system default programs that will allow you to create backups easily. Furthermore, you might want to schedule automatic backups so you can free your mind from having to remember to do it periodically.
  2. Cloud: Increasingly, there are free and commercial services to store your files and backups “in the cloud.” We will discuss “the cloud” in class during the course. For now, think of it as a flash drive located remotely and accessible via the Internet. Services such as Microsoft One Drive, Google Drive, Dropbox, or Apple iCloud are amongst the most popular. Installing the desktop version of these cloud services will provide you with automatic sync and backup of your files. Using these automated services gives you peace of mind and other benefits, such as being able to restore previous versions of stored files.
